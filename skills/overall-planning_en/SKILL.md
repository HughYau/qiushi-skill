# Overall Planning and Balanced Consideration

> "These ten questions are all raised around one fundamental policy: to mobilize all positive factors, domestic and international, to serve the cause of socialism."
> — Mao Zedong, "On the Ten Major Relationships"《论十大关系》(1956)

## Core Principle

**In complex systems, multiple dialectical relationships exist. You cannot attend to one end while neglecting the other. The correct method is to identify all important relationship pairs, find a dynamic balance among them, and mobilize all positive factors to the greatest extent possible.**

> See original-texts.md for detailed source references.

## When NOT to Use

**This skill is unnecessary in the following situations:**
- There is only one objective dimension; no opposing side needs balancing
- The user has already made an explicit trade-off decision (user decisions take priority)
- You are in the tactical execution phase where the overall direction is set and concentrated push is needed — use `concentrate-forces`
- The nature of the problem is a hard constraint where "one must be chosen and the other abandoned," leaving no room for dynamic balance

## When to Use

Invoke this skill when:

- Facing multiple mutually constraining goals that require trade-off decisions
- Discovering that optimizing one dimension is damaging another
- Multiple stakeholders exist in the system and coordination is needed
- Choosing between opposites such as speed vs. quality, depth vs. breadth, short-term vs. long-term
- Needing to formulate a comprehensive plan that addresses multiple dimensions
- Realizing you are one-sidedly pursuing a single goal

## Method Workflow

### Step 1: Identify All Dialectical Relationship Pairs

> Mao Zedong systematically identified ten pairs of relationships in "On the Ten Major Relationships"《论十大关系》

List all important opposites in the current system or task:

**Common dialectical relationship pairs:**
- Speed vs. Quality
- Depth vs. Breadth
- Short-term gains vs. Long-term value
- Simplicity vs. Flexibility
- Core features vs. Additional features
- Individual needs vs. Overall interests
- Exploration and innovation vs. Stability and reliability
- Centralized control vs. Distributed autonomy
- Theoretical study vs. Practical operation
- Known solutions vs. Innovative experiments

**Note:** Different tasks and systems have different dialectical relationship pairs. Identify them based on the specific situation; do not mechanically copy templates.

### Step 2: Reject One-Sidedness

> The lesson of the Soviet Union was excessive bias toward one side — "On the Ten Major Relationships"《论十大关系》

Examine whether you are one-sidedly emphasizing a particular aspect:
- Are you pursuing only speed while neglecting quality?
- Are you focusing only on the immediate while neglecting the long term?
- Are you satisfying one party's needs while ignoring others?
- Are you copying someone else's approach without considering your own actual conditions?

**Key test:** If what you are doing completely ignores one side of a relationship pair, that is one-sidedness.

### Step 3: Seek Dynamic Balance

> "Overall planning and balanced consideration; appropriate arrangements" — "On the Correct Handling of Contradictions Among the People"《关于正确处理人民内部矛盾的问题》

Balance is not a fixed compromise but a dynamic adjustment based on specific conditions:
- At the current stage, which side needs more attention?
- After conditions change, does the balance point need adjustment?
- "Appropriate arrangements" — must consider "the actual conditions possible at the given time and place"

**Method:**
1. For each relationship pair, determine the priority side for the current stage (without completely neglecting the other)
2. Set specific balance indicators (How do you determine that imbalance has occurred?)
3. Periodically check the balance state

### Step 4: Learn from Others' Lessons

> "They have already taken the detours — do you still want to follow?" — "On the Ten Major Relationships"《论十大关系》

Reference the failure lessons from similar scenarios:
- Which projects failed because of one-sidedly pursuing a single dimension?
- What common "over-optimization" traps exist?
- Are there mature balancing strategies that can be referenced?

But referencing is not copying — you must adapt to your own specific situation.

### Step 5: Mobilize All Positive Factors

> "Mobilize all positive factors, domestic and international" — "On the Ten Major Relationships"《论十大关系》

The ultimate goal of overall planning is not a compromise of "a little less of everything" but finding a configuration that **lets all aspects play a positive role**:
- Is there a way to advance two seemingly contradictory goals simultaneously?
- Are there neglected resources or methods that can be utilized?
- Is there a creative solution that can accommodate multiple parties?
- The possibility of transforming "negative factors" into "positive factors"

### Step 6: Systems Thinking

When adjusting one relationship pair, check the impact on other relationships:
- If speed is increased, is quality compromised?
- If depth is increased, is breadth insufficient?
- If short-term needs are met, are long-term risks being buried?

**Every adjustment must be checked for its global impact.**

## Common Dialectical Relationship Handling Patterns

| Relationship Pair | One-Sided Approach | Overall Planning Approach |
|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Speed vs. Quality | Only rushing deadlines, ignoring quality | Strictly gatekeep at critical points; appropriately accelerate at non-critical ones |
| Depth vs. Breadth | Going down rabbit holes or staying superficial | First survey broadly, then go deep at key points |
| Short-term vs. Long-term | Only seeing the immediate or being overly ambitious | Consider long-term impact while solving immediate problems |
| Simplicity vs. Flexibility | Over-simplifying or over-engineering | Sufficient for now, with room reserved for extension |
| Exploration vs. Stability | Afraid to innovate or taking excessive risks | Explore methodically on a stable foundation |

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Mao Zedong's Critique | Correct Approach |
|---------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Attending to only one end | One-sidedness is the most common error | Identify all important relationships; reject one-sidedness |
| Fixed compromise | Balance is dynamic, not fixed | Dynamically adjust the balance point based on conditions |
| Copying others' solutions | "They have already taken the detours — do you still want to follow?" | Reference but do not copy; adapt to your actual situation |
| Adjusting one area without considering the whole | Systems thinking is required | Check global impact with every adjustment |
| Doing a bit of everything (watering things down) | "Mobilize all positive factors" ≠ equal distribution | Find the optimal configuration where all aspects play their role |

## Operating Procedure

When this skill is triggered, **output a dialectical relationship panorama and balance plan**:

1. **Identify all dialectical relationship pairs**, format:
   ```
   Dialectical relationships involved:
   1. [A] ↔ [B]: Currently biased toward [A/B/balanced], degree of bias: [slight/noticeable/severe]
   2. [C] ↔ [D]: Currently biased toward [C/D/balanced], degree of bias: ...
   ```

2. **One-sidedness check** (for each relationship pair):
   - Does the current plan or action completely ignore one side?
   - If yes: Is this a deliberate trade-off (acceptable) or an unintentional neglect (needs correction)?

3. **Determine the balance point for the current stage** (give a specific priority direction for each pair):
   ```
   [A] vs [B]: Current stage prioritizes [A], but ensuring [B] does not fall below [minimum threshold]
   Reason: ... (related to the specific conditions of the current stage)
   ```

4. **System impact check** (after adjusting one relationship pair, check impact on others):
   ```
   If [A] is strengthened, impact on other relationships:
   - On [C] vs [D]: [Worsens/Alleviates/No impact], adjustment needed: ...
   ```

5. **Set imbalance warning indicators**:
   ```
   If [specific signal] appears, it indicates [A] vs [B] has become imbalanced; re-invoke this skill
   ```

## Relationships with Other Skills

- **Contradiction Analysis**: The process of identifying dialectical relationship pairs is contradiction analysis
- **Investigation First**: Understanding the actual situation on all fronts is the prerequisite for overall planning
- **Mass Line**: Overall planning requires listening to all parties' opinions
- **Concentrate Forces**: After overall planning sets the general direction, concentrate forces at specific execution points
- **Protracted Strategy**: Different stages have different balance points, requiring stage-by-stage adjustments
